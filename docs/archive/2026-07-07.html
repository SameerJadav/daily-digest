China conducts controversial long-range missile test in South Pacific

What happened

China's navy launched a long-range, nuclear-capable ballistic missile from a submarine into the South Pacific on Monday. The missile carried a simulated training warhead and successfully landed in a pre-arranged sea zone. Beijing only informed neighboring nations like Australia and New Zealand a few hours before the launch, sparking regional condemnation.

Why it matters

This is the first such test in two years. It occurred just hours after Australia and Fiji signed a new defense treaty. Some analysts view the test as an attempt to intimidate Pacific nations against building closer ties with Western allies. However, Chinese officials state the test was a routine annual training exercise and not aimed at any specific country.

What to watch next

Watch for responses from regional leaders at the NATO (the North Atlantic Treaty Organization, a Western military alliance) summit in Turkey this week, and monitor whether Pacific Island nations release joint statements protesting the test.

Words to know

A 1986 agreement that declared the South Pacific a nuclear-free zone, which China signed.

Canada selects German firm for historic multi-billion dollar submarine contract

What happened

Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney announced that German shipbuilder TKMS will build up to 12 new stealth submarines for Canada. TKMS won a highly competitive bidding war against South Korea's Hanwha Ocean. The first four diesel-electric submarines are expected to enter service by 2034, replacing Canada's decades-old fleet.

Why it matters

This is the largest military purchase in Canadian history, estimated to cost over 22 billion dollars for construction and up to 70 billion dollars with long-term maintenance. Canada has faced intense pressure from the US to increase its defense spending. The new submarines will help Canada secure its vast Arctic coastline, including the Northwest Passage, where melting ice is opening new shipping routes.

What to watch next

Watch for the official start of contract negotiations, which Germany's TKMS hopes to finalize by the end of 2026.

Words to know

Conventional submarine kun-VEN-shun-ul SUB-muh-reen
A submarine powered by diesel-electric engines rather than nuclear power.
Interoperability IN-tur-op-er-uh-BIL-ih-tee
The ability of different military forces and equipment to work together smoothly.

Emmanuel Macron visits post-Assad Syria in landmark diplomatic move

What happened

French President Emmanuel Macron arrived in Damascus on Monday to meet with Syria's new President, Ahmed al-Sharaa. This is the first visit by a Western European leader since former dictator Bashar al-Assad was overthrown in late 2024. Macron traveled with a delegation of top business executives to discuss security and rebuilding opportunities.

Why it matters

Syria's new president, al-Sharaa, was once a heavily sanctioned militant leader. Macron led the charge to lift Western sanctions on him, hoping to encourage stability in the war-torn country. Macron's visit signals a major shift in European diplomacy, though many Western governments remain cautious about how al-Sharaa's government treats ethnic and religious minorities.

What to watch next

Watch for a scheduled high-profile meeting between Syrian President al-Sharaa and US President Donald Trump during the NATO summit in Turkey on Tuesday, July 7.

Words to know

Hayat Tahrir al-Sham hah-YAT tah-REER ash-SHAHM
A Syrian militant group, formerly linked to al-Qaeda, whose leader is now the President of Syria.
Pluralist PLOOR-ul-ist
A system or society where diverse ethnic, religious, or political groups co-exist and share power.

Austrian court sentences former Syrian Assad regime officials for torture

What happened

A court in Vienna, Austria, sentenced two former Syrian officials under the ousted Assad government to eight years in prison. The lead defendant, Khaled al-Halabi, was a general who led intelligence services in the city of Raqqa, while Musab Abu Rukbah was a police investigator. They were convicted of torturing and abusing political opponents between 2011 and 2013.

Why it matters

The trial is a rare example of European courts prosecuting war crimes committed in foreign conflicts under the rule of universal jurisdiction. The two men had fled to Austria as refugees in 2015 under a secret agreement with European intelligence agencies. Witnesses traveled from across Europe to testify about enduring severe physical and psychological abuse under their custody.

What to watch next

Watch for whether the defense lawyers file appeals against the eight-year prison sentences in the coming weeks.

Words to know

Universal jurisdiction YOO-nih-vur-sul joor-is-DIK-shun
A legal rule that allows national courts to prosecute individuals for extremely serious international crimes, like torture, no matter where they happened.
Nuremberg defense NYOOR-um-burg dih-FENS
A legal argument where an accused person claims they were only following orders from superiors, which courts historically reject for war crimes.

Cuba hit by third nationwide power failure this year amid US oil blockade

What happened

Cuba suffered a complete collapse of its national electricity grid on Monday, plunging the island of 9.6 million people into darkness. This is the third nationwide blackout of the year, driven by a shortage of fuel to run the country's aging power stations. The shortage has been severely worsened by a US oil blockade imposed in January, which blocks global tankers from delivering fuel.

Why it matters

The blackouts have paralyzed daily life, forcing residents to survive on only a few hours of electricity per day and leaving some rural areas without power for days. Food, water, and medicines are running critically low, leading the UN (the United Nations) to warn of a humanitarian emergency. Even the Havana national zoo has resorted to using horse-drawn buggies to feed its animals because of strict fuel rationing.

What to watch next

Watch for updates on whether the Cuban government secures emergency fuel shipments from allies like Russia or China, and monitor the UN's response to the growing humanitarian crisis.

Words to know

Blockade bloh-KADE
An act of sealing off a place to prevent goods, especially fuel or food, from entering or leaving.
Soviet-era SOH-vee-et ER-uh
Referring to the period before 1991 when the Soviet Union existed, during which Cuba's current, decaying infrastructure was built.

Two soldiers killed in militant ambush in Manipur's Ukhrul district

What happened

Unidentified gunmen ambushed an Assam Rifles patrol in Manipur on Monday afternoon, killing two soldiers and injuring several others. The soldiers belonged to the 40 Assam Rifles detachment. Although some reports blamed a faction of the National Socialist Council of Nagalim (NSCN), the rebel group denied any involvement and stated it remains committed to its 1997 ceasefire agreement.

Why it matters

Manipur has been highly volatile, with ongoing ethnic conflicts and active militant groups operating in the region. Manipur Chief Minister Yumnam Khemchand Singh condemned the attack, stating the government will not tolerate such violence and will punish those responsible. The incident raises fears of renewed instability in India's northeast.

What to watch next

Watch for the results of the state police investigation and whether the Indian military increases its deployment in the Ukhrul district to track down the attackers.

Words to know

Assam Rifles AS-sam RY-fulz
India's oldest paramilitary force, responsible for border security and maintaining internal order in the northeastern states.
Ceasefire SEES-fyre
A temporary agreement to stop fighting, such as the one signed in 1997 between the Indian government and the NSCN.
