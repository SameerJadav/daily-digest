Andy Burnham confirmed as UK Labour leader and incoming Prime Minister

What happened

Britain's ruling Labour Party has officially confirmed Andy Burnham, the highly popular former Mayor of Greater Manchester, as its new leader. He will succeed Keir Starmer, who resigned in June amid controversy over an ambassador appointment. Since Labour holds a large majority in parliament, Burnham will automatically become the UK's next prime minister on Monday without requiring a general election.

Why it matters

Burnham, often called the 'King of the North,' represents a shift toward what he calls 'pro-business socialism,' promising to reverse decades of free-market policies by bringing utilities back into public ownership and investing heavily in social care and housing. However, he faces immediate internal party friction over his potential cabinet appointments, especially regarding the crucial role of Chancellor of the Exchequer, as left-wing factions clash with business-friendly moderates.

What to watch next

Watch for Keir Starmer to formally tender his resignation to King Charles III on Monday, July 20, 2026, followed by Burnham's official appointment and the announcement of his new cabinet members on the same day.

Words to know

by-election BY-ih-lek-shun
An election held to fill a single vacant seat in parliament between general elections. In this story, Burnham had to win a by-election to re-enter parliament before becoming party leader.
chancellor CHAN-suh-ler
The senior government official in charge of the country's treasury and economic policy. Burnham is facing intense party pressure over whom he chooses for this role.

UPDATE: US-Iran conflict escalates to direct mainland infrastructure strikes and base attacks

What happened

The US-Iran conflict has escalated to direct state-on-state warfare as US forces launched a seventh night of airstrikes directly targeting Iranian civilian and military infrastructure on the mainland, including bridges, energy facilities, and a commercial port control tower. In response, Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps fired missiles and drones directly at US military bases in allied regional nations—including Bahrain, Kuwait, Jordan, Oman, and Qatar—damaging a vital water desalination plant in Kuwait and reportedly hitting US aircraft in Jordan. This escalation comes after the US reinstated its naval blockade on Iran and Iran closed the Strait of Hormuz, with the US recently disabling a commercial tanker.

Why it matters

This marks a dangerous shift from maritime skirmishes to direct attacks on mainland infrastructure, raising concerns of potential war crimes over the targeting of civilian power grids and water supplies. The severe escalation has completely shattered last month's fragile maritime truce and has already driven global oil prices up by 10% this week.

What to watch next

We were previously watching for Iran's response to the disabling of the Belma and whether regional Gulf states would seek to mediate. Now, monitor whether the UN Security Council or regional powers convene an emergency dialogue to halt the strikes, and watch for official US Central Command statements regarding the damage to its aircraft and bases in Jordan and Qatar.

Words to know

desalination dee-sal-ih-NAY-shun
The chemical process of removing salt and other minerals from saline water to make it fit for human consumption. Kuwait relies on a desalination plant damaged in the Iranian strikes for 90% of its drinking water.

European Union proposes relaxing carbon market rules to slow emission cuts for businesses

What happened

The European Commission has proposed a major overhaul of its flagship Emissions Trading System that would relax carbon-cutting rules and give industries more time to reduce their greenhouse gas emissions. The plan would extend free pollution permits for heavy industries, like steel and cement, until 2038 instead of phasing them out by 2034 as originally scheduled.

Why it matters

The trading system is the EU's primary tool to combat climate change, credited with halving industrial emissions since 2005. However, under pressure from member states worried about soaring energy costs due to the war in the Middle East and competition from heavily subsidized foreign rivals, the EU is prioritizing business survival. Climate activists and Green politicians warn that slowing down these limits risks undermining Europe's goal of reducing emissions by 90% by 2040.

What to watch next

Watch for the year-long negotiations between EU member states and European Parliament lawmakers to finalize and vote on the revised carbon rules.

Words to know

emissions ee-MISH-unz
The release of greenhouse gases, such as carbon dioxide, into the atmosphere from industrial activities. The EU's market caps these emissions to combat global warming.
decarbonisation dee-kar-buh-ny-ZAY-shun
The process of reducing or eliminating carbon dioxide emissions from an economy or industry. The EU will offer upfront permits to businesses that commit to investing in decarbonisation.

Japan relaxes imperial succession rules but maintains ban on female monarchs

What happened

Japan's parliament has passed a landmark bill to reform its imperial succession laws to prevent the world's oldest continuous hereditary monarchy from dying out. The changes allow imperial princesses to retain their royal status after marrying commoners and permit the family to adopt unmarried male descendants from historical royal branches. However, the law maintains a strict ban on women ascending the Chrysanthemum Throne.

Why it matters

The imperial family has dwindled significantly, leaving 19-year-old Prince Hisahito as the only remaining young male heir under 60. While the reforms aim to secure the family's future, they run counter to public opinion, as polls show over 70% of Japanese citizens support allowing Emperor Naruhito's popular daughter, Princess Aiko, to inherit the throne. Critics argue that retaining the male-only rule perpetuates gender discrimination and relies on complex adoption schemes rather than simpler, highly supported reforms.

What to watch next

Watch for the final administrative procedures to be completed before the new laws officially take effect, and monitor whether the public's dissatisfaction with the exclusion of Princess Aiko impacts support for Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi's conservative government.

Words to know

patrilineal pah-trih-LIN-ee-ul
Relating to or tracing descent through the male line. Japan's imperial system maintains a strictly patrilineal succession model.
commoner KOM-uh-ner
A person who is not a member of the royal family or nobility. Under the new rules, princesses will no longer lose their royal status when they marry commoners.

Suspected Somali pirates hijack second tanker off Yemen, raising fears of resurgence

What happened

Armed men, identified by Somali security officials as pirates, hijacked a Tanzanian-flagged oil tanker, the MT Asana, off the coast of Yemen in the Gulf of Aden. The vessel was boarded by seven gunmen who set off from a remote area in Somalia's semi-autonomous Puntland region.

Why it matters

This is the second hijacking of a commercial tanker in the region in three months, following a decade of relative calm after intensive international naval patrols. Analysts warn that Somali piracy is resurging because international naval forces are currently stretched thin, having been redirected to handle the ongoing US-Iran conflict in the Red Sea and the Strait of Hormuz.

What to watch next

Watch for whether the Yemeni coast guard and international maritime partners successfully intercept the hijacked tanker as it sails toward Somalia, and monitor negotiations for the release of the crew.

Words to know

semi-autonomous sem-ee-aw-TON-uh-muss
Having a significant degree of self-government but remaining part of a larger state. Puntland is a semi-autonomous region in northeastern Somalia where the pirates originated.
ransom RAN-sum
A sum of money demanded or paid for the release of a captive. Pirates historically targeted ships to extort millions of dollars in ransom payments.

India launches its first hydrogen-powered train on the Jind-Sonipat route

What happened

Prime Minister Narendra Modi has flagged off India’s first hydrogen-powered train, marking a major milestone in the country's push for green transportation. Developed indigenously by the Integral Coach Factory in Chennai, the 10-car train set will operate on an 89-kilometer-long pilot route between Jind and Sonipat in Haryana, stopping at 12 stations.

Why it matters

Hydrogen-powered trains emit only water vapor and steam, making them a highly clean alternative to traditional diesel locomotives. By launching this service, India joins a small group of advanced nations—including Germany, China, Japan, the US, and Switzerland—actively deploying hydrogen technology to decarbonize their railway networks.

What to watch next

Watch for Northern Railway to release official operational and performance data from the daily runs of the Jind-Sonipat service, and track when the Ministry of Railways announces plans to expand hydrogen trains to other non-electrified routes.

Words to know

indigenously in-DIH-juh-nus-lee
Naturally occurring or produced locally in a particular country rather than imported. The hydrogen train was developed and built indigenously in India.
locomotive loh-kuh-MOH-tiv
A powered railway vehicle used for pulling trains. Hydrogen technology offers a clean alternative to diesel-powered locomotives.
