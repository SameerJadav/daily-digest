Deadly wildfire sweeps through southern Spain amid intense heatwave

What happened

A fast-moving wildfire in the southeastern Spanish province of Almería has killed at least 12 people and left 23 missing. The fire broke out on Thursday near the village of Bédar, consuming over 3,200 hectares of land. Emergency officials said many victims died while trying to escape in cars and on foot, ignoring advice to stay in place, and became trapped in a dry riverbed.

Why it matters

This is being described as one of Spain's deadliest wildfires of the century. Climate scientists warn that rising temperatures are making southern Europe's landscapes highly flammable, while rural flight has left land untended, allowing overgrown plants to build up massive fuel loads. High winds and a series of heatwaves have combined to make firefighting exceptionally difficult.

What to watch next

Watch for Spanish emergency services to release the official DNA identification of the victims, and monitor whether the wildfire risk level drops as firefighters attempt to fully contain the blaze by the weekend.

A province in southeastern Spain known for its semi-arid climate, where the recent deadly wildfire broke out.

The physical shape and features of an area of land. In this story, the steep and rugged terrain made it very difficult for heavy firefighting machines to access the fire.
expats EX-pats
Short for expatriates, meaning people who live outside their native countries. Many of the wildfire victims in Spain were foreign nationals.

Violent protests and lynchings erupt in West Bengal after schoolgirl's murder

What happened

Violent protests have shaken West Bengal after the rape and murder of an 11-year-old girl outside Kolkata. Local residents, angry at the crime, beat an innocent man to death because they suspected him of the crime, and police later shot and killed the main suspect during what they claimed was an escape attempt. Civil rights groups have questioned this police killing, calling for an independent judicial investigation, while local political parties have clashed over the state's security.

Why it matters

This incident has sparked a major political battle in West Bengal. The newly elected state government, led by the Bharatiya Janata Party, is facing fierce criticism from opposition parties over its handling of law and order. Observers fear the situation is being politicized, with some leaders highlighting religious differences between the Muslim victim and the Hindu suspects to rally support.

What to watch next

Watch for the Calcutta High Court to respond to demands from civil rights groups for an independent judicial probe, and monitor whether Chief Minister Suvendu Adhikari's planned visit to the area next week calms or worsens local tensions.

Words to know

encounter killing en-KOWN-ter KIL-ing
A term used in India for an incident where police shoot and kill a criminal suspect, often claiming they acted in self-defense. Critics frequently accuse police of staging these killings to bypass the legal system.
lynching LIN-ching
An extrajudicial killing, usually by an angry mob, to punish an individual for an alleged offense without a legal trial.
autopsy AW-top-see
A surgical examination of a dead body to determine the cause of death.

Developing nations spend more on foreign debt than education, UN reports

What happened

A new report by UNESCO, the UN's education and cultural agency, shows that 113 developing countries spent more on repaying foreign debt than on education in 2025. In sub-Saharan Africa, countries spent 3.6 times more on debt servicing than on schooling. The UN warns that international aid for education is also falling sharply, which could trap poorer countries in a cycle of underfunded schools, unpaid teachers, and stalled economic growth.

Why it matters

High debt levels prevent developing nations from investing in their youth and future workforces. Shocks from the COVID-19 pandemic, rising interest rates, and climate disasters have ballooned debt payments to a 35-year high. Campaign groups are calling for international debt relief, warning that private lenders often block relief agreements to protect their profits.

What to watch next

Watch for discussions on debt relief at international forums, particularly whether the UK uses its upcoming presidency of the G20 in 2027 to reform debt laws.

Words to know

UNESCO yoo-NES-koh
The United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization, a specialized agency aimed at promoting world peace and security through international cooperation.
debt servicing det SER-viss-ing
The payment of interest and principal on outstanding loans.
G20 jee-TWEN-tee
An international group of 19 countries plus the European Union and the African Union, representing the world's major economies, which meets to discuss global financial stability and development.

Trump administration rolls back crucial US environmental protections

What happened

The US Trump administration has repealed a key regulation under the Endangered Species Act, removing habitat protections for threatened wildlife. For 50 years, the law defined "harm" to include destroying the habitats that plants and animals need to survive. The administration rescinded this definition, claiming it was a regulatory burden that interfered with private property rights.

Why it matters

Environmentalists call the decision a "death sentence" for vulnerable species like monarch butterflies and Florida manatees, as habitat destruction is the biggest driver of species loss. The move is part of a broader push by President Donald Trump to deregulate US industries, opening up sensitive natural areas to logging, mining, and oil extraction. While the administration says directly killing wildlife remains illegal, experts warn that destroying their homes will lead to the same result.

What to watch next

Watch for environmental groups to file lawsuits in US federal courts to block the rule change, and monitor whether the "God squad" panel approves new oil and gas operations in the Gulf of Mexico.

Words to know

Endangered Species Act en-DAYN-jerd SPEE-sheez akt
A landmark US environmental law passed in 1973 designed to protect critically imperiled species from extinction.
regulatory intrusion REG-yoo-luh-tor-ee in-TROO-zhun
Government rules or actions that private businesses or individuals feel interfere too much with their personal or commercial freedom.
God squad god skwod
An informal nickname for the high-level US committee of federal officials that has the legal power to exempt specific projects from the Endangered Species Act, potentially deciding whether a species survives or goes extinct.

European Union accuses Meta of breaching tech rules with "addictive" designs

What happened

The European Commission has announced preliminary findings that Meta, the parent company of Instagram and Facebook, is violating EU tech laws. The EU accuses Meta of deliberately designing its platforms with "addictive" features like infinite scroll, autoplay videos, and highly personalized algorithms that can harm the mental health of young users. Meta has disputed these findings, pointing to its new "Teen Accounts" features that give parents control over screen time.

Why it matters

This investigation falls under the EU's Digital Services Act, which aims to force major tech companies to protect users from online harms. If Meta fails to modify its apps, it could face massive fines of up to 6% of its global annual revenue. This challenge strikes at the core of Meta's business model, which relies on maximizing user engagement to sell advertisements.

What to watch next

Watch for Meta's formal response to the EU's preliminary charges in the coming months, and monitor a key expert panel meeting on Monday, July 13, 2026, where the EU will discuss potential recommendations on shielding children online.

Words to know

European Commission yoor-uh-PEE-un... kuh-MISH-un
The executive branch of the European Union, responsible for proposing legislation, enforcing EU laws, and running the daily operations of the bloc.
Digital Services Act DIJ-i-tul SER-viss-ez akt
A major EU law that regulates online platforms, requiring them to quickly remove illegal content, combat disinformation, and protect users' rights.
infinite scroll IN-fi-nit skrohl
A design feature on websites and social media apps that continuously loads new content as a user scrolls down, preventing them from reaching a natural stopping point.

Nigerian military rescues 46 abducted school children and teachers

What happened

Nigerian security forces have rescued 46 school children and teachers who were kidnapped nearly two months ago from three schools in the southwestern state of Oyo. President Bola Tinubu announced that the military-led rescue operation killed several militants and arrested eight others from the Ansaru militant group. The government stated that no ransom was paid to secure the hostages' release, though one teacher was unfortunately killed while in captivity.

Why it matters

While school kidnappings by armed groups seeking ransom are common in northern Nigeria, this mass abduction took place in the southwest, raising fears that the country's security crisis is spreading to previously peaceful regions. The abduction had sparked local protests and a month-long teachers' strike. However, over 40 other children abducted on the same day in northeastern Borno state remain in captivity.

What to watch next

Watch for the state government to reunite the rescued students with their families in Oyo, and monitor whether the military launches new search operations to find the missing children in Borno state.

Words to know

Boko Haram BOH-koh huh-RAHM
An Islamist militant group based in northeastern Nigeria that has carried out widespread violence, bombings, and mass kidnappings since 2009.
Ansaru an-SAH-roo
An extremist militant group in Nigeria that split from Boko Haram and is known for targeting foreigners and security forces.
Oyo AW-yoh
A state located in southwestern Nigeria, traditionally considered safer than the conflict-torn northern regions.
