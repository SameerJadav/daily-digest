UPDATE: Indian activist Sonam Wangchuk ends 26-day hunger strike after government promises exam leak reforms

What happened

Indian activist Sonam Wangchuk ended his 26-day hunger strike late on Thursday after the government agreed to drop charges against peaceful student protesters. The administration also agreed to consider financial compensation for the families of students who committed suicide following recent exam leaks. This decision came as Prime Minister Narendra Modi promised a strict new law and special fast-track courts to punish those behind the leaks, while replacing Higher Education Secretary Vineet Joshi. We were previously watching for how the government would address the massive youth-led protests over leaked medical entrance exams.

Why it matters

The ongoing student protests had paralyzed parts of Delhi and drawn intense support from major opposition parties. By quickly offering concessions and shifting top bureaucrats, the government hopes to defuse a political crisis that threatened to disrupt the current academic year for millions of students. However, protest leaders argue that these measures still fail to address direct ministerial accountability or their demand for the education minister's resignation.

What to watch next

Watch for the Indian Cabinet to discuss the draft anti-leak bill on Friday, July 24, 2026, and track when the government introduces the bill in Parliament next week. Monitor whether protest leaders hold scheduled meetings with Union Ministers J.P. Nadda and Jitendra Singh to negotiate further reforms.

The committee of senior government ministers who are responsible for controlling government policy and making major national decisions.

A specialized legal court established to hear and resolve specific criminal cases much more quickly than the standard judicial system.

UPDATE: US threatens massive strikes on Iran as Houthi shipping attacks push oil past $100

What happened

US President Donald Trump announced he is close to launching a massive military attack on Iran after Houthi rebels struck two Saudi Arabian oil tankers in the Red Sea. The escalating conflict has pushed global Brent crude oil prices past $100 a barrel for the first time in two months, threatening to trigger fresh global inflation. Meanwhile, Kuwait's military reported intercepting drone and missile strikes from Iran, and Germany announced it is withdrawing two navy ships from the Red Sea due to the volatile security situation. We were previously watching for whether the US would escalate its military action following the Houthi naval blockade of the Bab el-Mandeb strait.

Why it matters

The Houthi attacks on Saudi tankers have targeted the Red Sea, a vital maritime trade route, at a time when the Strait of Hormuz—the world's most critical oil chokepoint—is already effectively closed by Iran. This double threat could paralyze global energy shipments and trigger a severe global economic shock, leading investors to dump government bonds and stocks. The widening war has also drawn in neighboring states like Kuwait and prompted European nations to pull back their naval forces, raising fears of an uncontrolled regional conflict.

What to watch next

Watch for a formal decision by President Trump on whether to launch the threatened massive airstrikes against Iran, and track if Israel joins the military action. Monitor whether Germany's navy successfully relocates its two vessels, the Fulda and the Mosel, to the eastern Mediterranean in the coming days.

UPDATE: US imposes sweeping forced-labor tariffs on over 80 nations, triggering retaliatory threats

What happened

The Trump administration has announced new tariffs of 10% to 12.5% on 86 nations, including India, China, Canada, Mexico, the UK, and the European Union, starting Friday. The US Trade Representative claimed these countries failed to effectively ban and block imports made with forced labor. This massive new wave of taxes replaces a temporary 10% global tariff that was set to expire on Friday morning. In response, Brazil rejected the claims as arbitrary and unjustified and announced immediate plans to slap retaliatory tariffs on US goods. We were previously watching for how countries like Brazil and Canada would respond to targeted US tariffs.

Why it matters

These tariffs are President Trump's latest effort to rebuild his aggressive trade policy after the US Supreme Court struck down his previous reciprocal tariffs in February. By using Section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974, which targets unfair trade practices, the US administration is trying to establish a more legally durable system of near-global import taxes. However, economists warn that penalizing nearly all of America's major trading partners at once risks escalating global trade wars, raising prices for US consumers, and driving up inflation.

What to watch next

Watch for the new tariffs to officially take effect on Friday, July 24, 2026, and track which US goods Brazil targets with its retaliatory duties. Monitor whether other major affected economies, such as India, the EU, or Canada, file formal challenges with the World Trade Organization or announce their own retaliatory taxes.

Words to know

Forced labor forst LAY-bur
Work or service that people are forced to do against their will under threat of punishment, which the US bans to protect human rights and fair trade.
Section 301 SEK-shun three-oh-wun
A provision of the US Trade Act of 1974 that allows the US president to impose tariffs or trade sanctions on countries that engage in unfair trade practices.
Supreme Court soo-PREEM kort
The highest judicial court in a country, which has the ultimate authority to interpret laws and decide if government actions are constitutional.

Massive wildfires in southwest France force evacuation of over 20,000 people

What happened

Raging wildfires in the Gironde region of southwest France have forced more than 20,000 residents and tourists to evacuate from homes, campsites, and holiday resorts near the Bay of Arcachon. Fueled by high winds and temperatures exceeding 30 degrees Celsius, the fire has destroyed over 3,400 hectares of dense pine forest west of Bordeaux. About 800 firefighters, supported by soldiers and water-bombing planes sent by the European Union, are struggling to contain the erratic flames. No civilian casualties have been reported, though several houses have been burned and two firefighters died earlier this week in a separate nearby blaze.

Why it matters

This intense wildfire is part of a broader crisis across southern Europe, which has been battered by three successive summer heatwaves linked to climate change. Extremely dry soils, strong winds, and record-high temperatures—including a scorching 44.7 degrees Celsius in Spain—have made forests highly vulnerable to fires, which are burning twice as much European land this year than the historical average. The scale of the destruction has exhausted emergency resources, prompting France to seek international aid and raising fears that Dantesque or hellish fire scenarios will become more frequent.

What to watch next

Monitor weather forecasts to see if temperatures drop or winds calm, which would help firefighters stabilize the main fire fronts. Watch for local authorities to release findings on the exact cause of the fire, which is suspected to have been started by a brush-clearing machine. Also, track whether the European Union deploys additional emergency personnel or aircraft to assist other southern European nations, such as Spain and Italy, currently battling their own blazes.

Words to know

Marine heatwave muh-REEN HEET-wayv
An extended period of abnormally high water temperatures in an ocean or sea. In this story, it is affecting 80% of the Mediterranean Sea.
Gendarmes ZHAHN-dahrmz
Members of a French military force who are responsible for police duties among the civilian population, especially in rural areas.
Hectare HEK-tair
A metric unit of square measure equal to 10,000 square meters, or about 2.47 acres, commonly used to measure land area.

UK cancels army training in Kenya over dispute on prosecuting British soldiers

What happened

The British government has cancelled a major military training exercise in Kenya after Kenyan lawmakers refused to ratify a bilateral defense agreement. The dispute arose because Kenyan authorities want greater powers to prosecute British soldiers who commit serious crimes, such as murder or sexual abuse, while training in the country. In response, the UK Ministry of Defence announced that the 1st Battalion the Duke of Lancaster’s Regiment will now train in an alternative country outside Kenya.

Why it matters

The cancellation is a significant blow to a long-standing defense partnership, as the British Army has trained thousands of troops at its BATUK base near Nanyuki for decades, generating vital income for the local Kenyan economy. However, public anger in Kenya has grown over alleged human rights violations, environmental damage, and the high-profile 2012 death of Agnes Wanjiru, a local woman whose murder has been linked to a British soldier. By standing firm, Kenyan lawmakers are demanding equal legal accountability, signaling a shift in how post-colonial defense relationships are negotiated.

What to watch next

Watch for a legal hearing in November 2026, which will determine if British soldier Robert Purkiss will be sent to Kenya to face murder charges for the death of Agnes Wanjiru. Monitor whether the UK and Kenyan governments can negotiate a compromise on the defense agreement to allow British military training to resume.

Words to know

Jurisdiction joor-is-DIK-shun
The official power or legal authority to make legal decisions and judgments, in this case determining which country's courts can try British soldiers.
Ministry of Defence MIN-is-tree ov dih-FENS
The British government department responsible for implementing the defense policy of the United Kingdom and managing its armed forces.

Angolan court sentences two Russians to prison for spying and plotting coup

What happened

An Angolan court has sentenced two Russian men, Lev Lakshtanov and Igor Ratchin, to eight and eleven years in prison respectively for spying, terrorism, and attempting to plan a coup. Angolan prosecutors proved that the men were linked to Africa Politology, a Russian disinformation network formerly run by the Wagner mercenary group, and sought to influence Angola's upcoming presidential elections. The court also handed a two-year suspended sentence to an Angolan sports journalist, while acquitting the youth leader of Angola's largest opposition party.

Why it matters

Angola has historically maintained close ties with Moscow since its civil war, but its current president, João Lourenço, has shifted the country's foreign policy closer to the US and Europe. The convictions highlight growing concerns over Russia's expanding influence and disinformation campaigns across Africa, where Russian forces currently support several military regimes and leaders. By prosecuting these agents, Angola's government is signaling its determination to protect its elections and distance itself from Russian covert operations.

What to watch next

Watch for the Angolan appeals court to rule on the appeals filed by both the convicted Russian men and the prosecutors who are challenging the lighter sentences of the Angolan co-defendants. Monitor how the Russian embassy in Luanda formally responds to the prison sentences of its citizens.

Words to know

Disinformation dis-in-fer-MAY-shun
False or misleading information that is deliberately spread to deceive people or influence public opinion, often by foreign governments.
Suspended sentence suh-SPEN-did SEN-tenss
A legal punishment where a judge convicts a person but delays their prison time, allowing them to stay free as long as they do not commit another crime.
Mercenary MUR-suh-ner-ee
A professional soldier hired to fight in a foreign army or private conflict, operating for money rather than for a specific country.
