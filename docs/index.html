Masses of mourners gather in Iran for Ayatollah Khamenei's funeral

What happened

Huge crowds of mourners gathered in Tehran for the first two days of a six-day funeral procession for late Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei. Mourners braved a severe heatwave to view the coffin at the Grand Mosalla mosque, where authorities set up water misters and distributed cold drinks. Yesterday, Iran began these ceremonies, which will move to the city of Qom and then to Iraq before the final burial in Mashhad on Thursday. Readers were watching to see if his son and successor, Mojtaba Khamenei, would make his first public appearance; while he has not yet appeared, school students distributed photos of people posing with his portrait, keeping speculation high.

Why it matters

The funeral is the largest in Iran's recent history and is highly political. The government is using the massive turnout to project religious and political strength to the world and channel public anger toward the US and Israel. The transition of power to Mojtaba Khamenei remains highly sensitive, as he has yet to be seen in public since taking office.

What to watch next

Watch whether Mojtaba Khamenei makes his first public appearance when the funeral procession moves to the holy city of Qom on Tuesday, July 7, or to the Shia holy cities of Najaf and Karbala in Iraq on Wednesday, July 8.

Words to know

Grand Mosalla grahnd moh-SAH-luh
A massive, open-air prayer space and mosque complex in central Tehran used for major religious and state events.
Qom kohm
A city in Iran that is a major sacred center of Shia Islam and a hub for religious scholarship.
martyrdom MAR-ter-dum
The death of a person who is killed because of their religious or political beliefs. In Iran, the state uses this term to honor dead leaders and rally public support.

Ukraine targets Russian energy infrastructure with drone strikes near St. Petersburg

What happened

Ukrainian drone strikes hit a major oil terminal in St. Petersburg and the nearby port of Vysotsk, about 850 kilometers from the Ukrainian border. Ukraine's military also claimed to have hit a major naval base in Kronstadt. Yesterday, Ukraine launched strikes on Russian oil terminals in the region. Readers were watching for Russian retaliations and fuel export impacts; Russian President Vladimir Putin has dismissed these strikes as not critical but signed a new law to boost domestic fuel supplies amid shortages. Meanwhile, Russia claimed to have captured the strategic eastern Ukrainian transport hub of Kostiantynivka, but Ukrainian officials strongly denied this.

Why it matters

Ukraine is actively targeting Russia's energy infrastructure to drain the financial resources Moscow uses to fund the war. These strikes have disabled a significant portion of Russia's refining capacity, causing domestic petrol shortages. The dispute over Kostiantynivka is also crucial, as the town is part of Ukraine's core defensive line in the industrial Donetsk region.

What to watch next

Watch for whether Ukraine releases verified footage of the Kronstadt naval base damage, and monitor frontline reports in the coming days to see if Russian troops can establish verifiable control over Kostiantynivka.

Words to know

Kostiantynivka kos-tyahn-TIH-neev-kah
A heavily fortified industrial town in eastern Ukraine's Donetsk region that acts as a key transport hub.
Kronstadt KRON-shtat
A historic Russian seaport and naval base located on an island near St. Petersburg.
Vysotsk vih-SOTSK
A Russian port city on the Baltic Sea that handles fossil fuels, grain, and coal.

India details charges against 23 newly designated Pakistan-based terrorists

What happened

India's Home Ministry released detailed allegations against the 23 newly designated terrorists under the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA). The list includes individuals accused of coordinating drone-delivered weapons, managing tunnel infiltration routes, and conducting reconnaissance on sensitive sites like the Ram Temple in Ayodhya. Six of the designated individuals are Indian nationals who fled to Pakistan or Pakistan-occupied Kashmir. Yesterday, India added these names to its official terrorist list. While readers were watching for Pakistan's official response, the newly published list reveals deep concerns over cross-border drone smuggling.

Why it matters

These designations allow the Indian government to freeze assets and restrict the movement of individuals associated with militant groups like Lashkar-e-Taiba and Jaish-e-Mohammad. Highlighting details like a former Pakistan Army soldier's involvement and drone-based weapon smuggling is meant to build international pressure on Pakistan for harboring these operatives.

What to watch next

Watch for Pakistan's formal response to these specific accusations and whether India presents this dossier at upcoming international security forums to push for global sanctions against these individuals.

Words to know

UAPA yoo-ay-pee-ay
The Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act, an Indian anti-terror law that allows the government to designate individuals as terrorists and freeze their assets.
reconnaissance rih-KON-uh-sunss
Military observation of a region or site to gather information about enemy positions or strategic vulnerabilities.
Shura SHOO-ruh
An Arabic word for a consultative council or governing body, used here to refer to the leadership council of a militant group.

Violent protests erupt in Germany against far-right AfD national convention

What happened

Over 30,000 protesters clashed with police in the eastern German city of Erfurt on Saturday during the national convention of the far-right Alternative for Germany (AfD) party. Demonstrators blocked highways, glued themselves to tram tracks, and abseiled from bridges to prevent delegates from attending. Despite the disruption, around 540 delegates reached the venue to re-elect their co-leaders ahead of crucial regional elections.

Why it matters

The AfD has surged in popularity, particularly in eastern Germany, raising deep alarm among mainstream politicians and citizens who accuse the party of promoting racist, anti-democratic, and anti-immigrant policies. The controversy was heightened because the convention coincided with the 100th anniversary of a historic Nazi Party meeting held nearby, a link the AfD denies but critics call a deliberate provocation.

What to watch next

Watch the results of the party conference as it concludes on Sunday, July 5, and monitor the state elections in eastern Germany this September to see if the AfD wins power at the state level for the first time.

Words to know

Alternative for Germany ahl-ter-nah-TEEVE fur DOYCH-lahnt
Known as the AfD, this is a far-right, anti-immigration political party in Germany.
national convention NA-shuh-nul kun-VEN-shun
A formal meeting of a political party where delegates gather to elect leaders and set party policies.
Weimar VY-mar
A city near Erfurt, historically significant as the place where the democratic post-World War I German government was established, and where early Nazi meetings took place.

Sudanese city of El Obeid devastated by escalating drone strikes

What happened

The Sudanese city of El Obeid is facing a severe humanitarian crisis following a dramatic surge in drone strikes that killed more than 20 people over the weekend. The attacks, blamed on the paramilitary Rapid Support Forces (RSF), hit schools, hospitals, and fuel stations, cutting off the city's power grid. The UN human rights office reported at least 45 deaths in June, warning that another major human rights catastrophe is unfolding.

Why it matters

El Obeid is a highly strategic transport and military hub located between the RSF-controlled western Darfur region and government-held eastern areas. If the RSF captures the city, it could lead to a massive siege and atrocities similar to those seen in other captured cities. The escalation has prompted the UN to issue a red alert to world leaders to intervene and prevent potential ethnic cleansing or genocide.

What to watch next

Watch for any coordinated response or emergency sessions from the UN Human Rights Council following the UK-backed debate, and monitor whether the Sudanese Armed Forces can maintain their 50-kilometer defensive line as RSF forces gather.

Words to know

Rapid Support Forces RA-pid sup-PORT FOR-sez
A powerful Sudanese paramilitary group currently fighting the official Sudanese army for control of the country.
El Obeid el oh-BAYD
A major, strategic city of half a million people in central Sudan, currently hosting over 100,000 displaced refugees.
Kordofan kor-doh-FAHN
A large region in central Sudan where El Obeid is located, currently a primary battleground in the civil war.

Venezuela earthquake death toll approaches 3,000 as rescue efforts wind down

What happened

The death toll from the twin earthquakes that struck Venezuela on June 24 has risen to nearly 3,000, with over 16,500 people injured and up to 50,000 still missing. International rescue teams, including squads from the US, have begun winding down operations as the critical window for finding survivors has closed. Acting President Delcy Rodriguez defended the government's response against growing public anger over slow aid delivery and a lack of heavy clearing machinery.

Why it matters

This is one of the worst earthquake disasters in Latin American history, leaving more than 16,000 people homeless and destroying vital infrastructure in the coastal La Guaira region. The scale of the destruction and the sluggish state response have fueled immense domestic frustration, threatening political stability as citizens struggle in makeshift tent camps.

What to watch next

Watch for updated official casualty figures from the Venezuelan government and reports on how the UN and international agencies distribute long-term shelter and food aid to the thousands of homeless families.

Words to know

La Guaira lah GWY-rah
A major coastal port city and region in northern Venezuela that was hardest hit by the earthquakes.
aftershocks AF-ter-shoks
Smaller earthquakes that follow a larger initial earthquake in the same area as the displaced crust adjusts.
