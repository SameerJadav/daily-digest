US launches airstrikes on southern Iran following attacks on commercial tankers

What happened

The US military launched powerful airstrikes targeting Iranian air defenses, missile sites, and drone launchers in southern Iran on Tuesday. The strikes follow attacks on three commercial tankers in the strategic Strait of Hormuz, which the US blamed on Iran. In response, the US Treasury also revoked a temporary waiver that had allowed Iran to sell oil, effectively breaking a fragile month-old ceasefire agreement.

Why it matters

The Strait of Hormuz is a vital international waterway through which a large portion of the world's oil and liquefied natural gas passes. The breakdown of the ceasefire risks a return to direct war between the US and Iran, which began in late February. Iran claims the right to charge shipping fees to secure the passage, a position Western nations and regional neighbors like Qatar and Saudi Arabia reject.

What to watch next

Watch for any retaliatory military action from Iran, which has vowed to take decisive measures, and see if the US proceeds with a full wind-down of all Iranian oil transactions by the July 17 deadline.

Words to know

Strait of Hormuz STRAYT of hor-MOOZ
A narrow, highly strategic waterway between the Persian Gulf and the Gulf of Oman. It is a critical choke point through which much of the world's oil supply is shipped.
Centcom SENT-kom
Short for United States Central Command, the branch of the US military responsible for operations in the Middle East and Central Asia.
Memorandum of understanding mem-uh-RAN-dum of un-der-STAN-ding
A formal but non-binding agreement between two or more parties. Here, it refers to the June agreement between the US and Iran that temporarily halted hostilities.

Blasts rock Damascus during landmark visit by French President Macron

What happened

UPDATE: Two bombs exploded near a hotel housing French President Emmanuel Macron in Damascus on Tuesday, wounding at least 18 people. Macron was safe at the presidential palace during the blasts and proceeded with his scheduled meeting with Syrian President Ahmed al-Sharaa. This landmark visit, which began on Monday, marks the first time a Western leader has traveled to Syria since former dictator Bashar al-Assad was ousted in December 2024.

Why it matters

The reader was watching for meetings between Syrian and Western leaders, but these explosions highlight the fragile security environment the new Syrian government faces as it tries to project stability. Despite the attacks, France and Syria signed 15 economic agreements to rebuild war-torn infrastructure and officially agreed to restore diplomatic ties by appointing ambassadors.

What to watch next

Watch for the findings of the Syrian Interior Ministry's investigation into who planted the explosives, and monitor whether Syria secures more European investment despite these security threats.

Words to know

Improvised explosive device IM-pruh-vyzd ik-SPLOH-siv di-VYSE
A homemade bomb constructed and deployed in ways other than in conventional military action. It is often used by guerrilla or insurgent forces.
Civil society SIV-il suh-SY-uh-tee
Organizations, groups, and individuals operating outside of government and business, such as charities, community groups, and activists.

NATO summit opens in Turkey amid intense defense spending disputes

What happened

UPDATE: The NATO summit officially opened in Ankara on Tuesday, immediately exposing sharp divisions as US President Donald Trump criticized European allies for not spending enough on defense. Trump also renewed his controversial demand for the US to take control of Greenland, which Denmark quickly rejected. This comes after Turkey detained dozens of activists to secure the capital ahead of hosting its first NATO summit in 22 years.

Why it matters

The reader was watching for the summit to start and for major defense contracts to be announced. Leaders did highlight several massive contracts, including Canada’s multi-billion-dollar deal with Germany for stealth submarines. However, the alliance faces deep structural uncertainty as the US scales back the military resources it commits to protecting Europe, forcing European nations to take more responsibility for their own defense.

What to watch next

Watch for the release of the summit's final joint declaration on Wednesday, July 8, to see if all 32 members sign off on unified defense spending targets.

Words to know

Gross domestic product grohs duh-MES-tik PROD-ukt
The total monetary value of all goods and services produced within a country in a specific period, used here to measure how much nations spend on defense relative to their economy.
Autonomous territory aw-TAHN-uh-mus TER-uh-tor-ee
A region that has a degree of freedom from an external authority, but still officially remains part of a larger country, such as Greenland's relationship with Denmark.

India and Indonesia sign major defense and mineral agreements during PM Modi's visit

What happened

Prime India Minister Narendra Modi and Indonesian President Prabowo Subianto signed 14 landmark agreements in Jakarta on Tuesday. The deals include in-principle agreements for India to supply BrahMos supersonic cruise missiles and Astra air-to-air missiles to Indonesia. The two nations also agreed to cooperate on critical mineral extraction, joint port development, and establishing an Indian Institute of Management (IIM) Bangalore campus in Indonesia.

Why it matters

This visit strengthens India's strategic footprint in Southeast Asia and secures access to Indonesia's vast critical mineral reserves, including nickel and copper, which are essential for manufacturing modern electronics and batteries. The defense deals also mark a significant expansion of India's military exports to friendly nations in the Indo-Pacific, helping counter China's regional dominance.

What to watch next

Watch for the formal start of contract negotiations to determine the exact number of BrahMos and Astra missiles Indonesia will purchase, and monitor when construction begins on the new IIM campus in Malang.

Words to know

Supersonic soo-per-SAHN-ik
Speed that is faster than the speed of sound. The BrahMos cruise missile travels at this speed to evade enemy air defense systems.
Bintang Adipurna bin-TAHNG ah-dee-POOR-nah
Indonesia's highest civilian award, which was presented to Prime Minister Modi to honor his contribution to bilateral relations.

Hungary halts state-run news broadcasts to dismantle former leader's propaganda network

What happened

Hungary's main public television channel, M1, and its state radio station, Kossuth, abruptly suspended their regular news broadcasts on Tuesday afternoon. The shutdown was ordered by Hungary's new Prime Minister, Péter Magyar, who swept to power in April after promising to end government control over public information. Instead of news, the television channel displayed a message apologizing for lying for so long and promising independent reforms, while the radio broadcast classical music.

Why it matters

For 16 years, former Prime Minister Viktor Orbán tightly controlled about 80 percent of Hungary's media, transforming state-run outlets into official mouthpieces to attack political opponents and the European Union. Magyar's drastic move is a major step toward restoring press freedom in Hungary, though Orbán's party has condemned the shutdown as an act of political tyranny.

What to watch next

Watch for the announcement of Hungary's new independent broadcast authority and when M1 television resumes news programming under its new editorial team.

Words to know

Illiberal democracy ih-LIB-er-ul dih-MOK-ruh-see
A governing system where, although elections take place, citizens are cut off from knowledge about the activities of those who exercise real power because of a lack of civil liberties like press freedom.
Supermajority SOO-per-muh-jor-ih-tee
A large majority of votes or seats in a parliament, usually two-thirds, which allows a ruling party to change the constitution or pass laws without opposition support.

International Olympic Committee provisionally lifts suspension on Russian Olympic Committee

What happened

The International Olympic Committee (IOC) provisionally lifted its suspension of the Russian Olympic Committee on Tuesday. The suspension was originally imposed in 2023 because Russia had unilaterally absorbed sports councils from occupied regions of Ukraine. The IOC said the ban was lifted because Russia confirmed its sports body no longer includes those occupied territories, paving the way for Russian athletes to participate in qualifying events for the 2028 Los Angeles Olympics.

Why it matters

The decision is a significant step toward reintegrating Russia into global sports, though the IOC has not yet decided whether Russian athletes will be allowed to use their national flag, colors, or anthem. The move has sparked controversy because Russia continues its military invasion of Ukraine, but the IOC argued that individual athletes should not be held responsible for the actions of their governments.

What to watch next

Watch for an official response from Ukraine's government or sports bodies, and look for a future IOC decision regarding whether Russian flags and anthems will be allowed at the 2028 Games.

Words to know

Neutral status NOO-trul STAY-tus
A designation allowing athletes to compete in international sports without officially representing any country, meaning they cannot display their national flag or play their anthem.
Doping controls DOH-ping kun-TROHLZ
Systemic testing of athletes' blood or urine to detect performance-enhancing drugs, which Russian athletes must undergo to regain trust following past state-sponsored cheating scandals.

Ghana postpones South African president's visit as anti-migrant diplomatic row widens

What happened

UPDATE: Ghana has indefinitely postponed a scheduled visit by South African President Cyril Ramaphosa because of intense public anger over xenophobic protests in South Africa. Ghana has already repatriated more than 900 of its citizens from the country and plans to bring back 900 more. This development broadens a diplomatic crisis that began last week when Nigeria strongly condemned South Africa over the deaths of its citizens in anti-migrant violence.

Why it matters

The reader was watching for diplomatic responses or African Union intervention. Instead, the regional fallout has widened, with multiple nations now flying their citizens home out of safety concerns. Many African countries are frustrated that South Africans are targeting fellow Africans, pointing out that their nations supported South Africans during their decades-long struggle to end minority rule.

What to watch next

Watch for whether the African Union convenes an emergency session or sends a fact-finding mission to South Africa to investigate the recurring attacks on foreign nationals.

Words to know

Bilateral meeting by-LAT-er-ul MEE-ting
A formal discussion or negotiation held between representatives of exactly two countries to discuss mutual concerns.
Diplomatic channels dip-luh-MAT-ik CHAN-ulz
The official means of communication between governments, typically through embassies and ambassadors, to resolve disputes quietly.
